## What Is A Home Network?

A home network is simply a group of devices connected together so they can communicate and access the internet.

Your home network probably already includes:
- phones
- laptops
- desktop computers
- smart TVs
- streaming devices
- tablets
- gaming consoles
- printers
- smart speakers
- security cameras

Most of these devices connect through your Wi-Fi router.

The router acts like a traffic manager for your home internet connection.

It helps devices:
- talk to each other
- reach websites
- send information
- receive information safely

Without a router, most home devices would not be able to share the internet connection properly.

---

## Think Of Your Network Like A Neighborhood

Here is a simple way to think about it:

- Your home network = your digital neighborhood
- Your router = the neighborhood traffic officer
- Your devices = the homes inside the neighborhood
- The internet = the rest of the world outside the neighborhood
- IP addresses = mailing addresses for devices so data knows where to go

Every connected device needs its own address so information knows where to go.

That address is called an IP address.

We will explore that more later.

## The Difference Between Wi-Fi And The Internet

Many people use the terms "Wi-Fi" and "internet" as if they mean the same thing.

They are related, but they are not the same thing.

### Wi-Fi

Wi-Fi is the wireless connection inside your home.

It allows devices like phones, laptops, and TVs to connect to your router without cables.

Wi-Fi only handles the local connection between your device and the router.

### Internet

The internet is the massive worldwide network outside your home.

Your router connects your home network to the internet through your Internet Service Provider (ISP).

Examples of ISPs include:
- Comcast
- Spectrum
- AT&T
- Verizon
- Cox
- Frontier

Your ISP is the company bringing internet access into your house.

---

## A Simple Way To Think About It

Here is an easy comparison:

- Wi-Fi = the road inside your neighborhood
- Internet = the entire highway system across the world

Your devices first connect to your Wi-Fi router.

Then your router connects outward to the internet.

## What Is An IP Address?

Every device connected to a network needs an address.

That address is called an IP address.

IP stands for:

**Internet Protocol**

An IP address allows devices to find and communicate with each other.

Without IP addresses:
- websites would not know where to send information
- devices could not communicate properly
- internet traffic would become chaos

---

## Think Of It Like Mailing A Letter

Imagine sending a package through the mail.

The package needs:
- a destination
- a return address
- a system that knows where to deliver it

Networking works in a very similar way.

Devices send information back and forth using IP addresses.

The address tells the network where the information should go.

---

## Private IP Addresses

Inside your home, devices usually receive a private IP address from your router.

These often look something like:
- 192.168.1.1
- 192.168.0.5
- 10.0.0.12

You do not need to memorize these numbers.

Just recognize that these are internal addresses used inside home networks.

Your router assigns these automatically.

---

## Public IP Address

Your home network also has a public IP address.

This is the address your internet provider assigns to your home connection.

Think of it like this:

- Private IP = room number inside a building
- Public IP = street address of the building itself

Websites on the internet usually see your public IP address, not the private address of individual devices inside your home.

## How To Find Your Router Address On Windows

1. Press the Windows key.
2. Type:

   ```bash
   cmd
   ```

3. Open Command Prompt.
4. Type:

   ```bash
   ipconfig
   ```

5. Press Enter.
6. Look for:

   ```text
   Default Gateway
   ```

The number next to **Default Gateway** is usually your router's IP address.

Example:

```text
Default Gateway . . . . . : 192.168.1.1
```

---

## How To Find Your Router Address On Linux

Open a terminal and type:

```bash
ip route
```

Look for the word:

```text
default
```

Example:

```text
default via 192.168.1.1 dev wlan0
```

The number shown after **default via** is usually your router address.

---

## How To Access The Router Login Page

Now that you have found your router address, you can try opening the router login page.

This is where many routers allow you to view basic network information and settings.

1. Open a web browser.
2. Click the address bar at the top.
3. Type your router address.

Example:

```text
http://192.168.1.1
```

4. Press Enter.

You may see a login screen.

That is normal.

---

## What If The Page Does Not Open?

- the router uses a different address

- the router uses a mobile app instead
- you are connected to the wrong network
- the router login page uses `https` instead of `http`

You can also try:

```text

```

If the page still does not open, that is okay.

You are still learning how to find and understand your network information.

## What Is DNS?

DNS stands for:

```text
Domain Name System
```

That sounds technical, but the idea is simple.

DNS helps turn website names into addresses computers can understand.

When you type a website like:

```text
google.com
```

your computer needs to know where that website lives on the internet.

DNS helps find the correct address.

---

## Think Of DNS Like A Phone Book

Most people do not memorize phone numbers anymore.

They tap a name in their contacts, and the phone finds the number.

DNS works in a similar way.

You type a website name.

DNS helps find the correct internet address for that website.

Without DNS, you would have to remember long numbers instead of easy website names.

---

## Why DNS Matters For Cybersecurity

DNS matters because almost everything you do online uses it.

When DNS is working normally, websites load the way you expect.

When DNS is abused, people can be sent to unsafe or fake websites.

This is one reason cybersecurity tools often pay attention to DNS activity.

DNS can help show:
- what websites devices are trying to reach
- whether a device is connecting to something suspicious
- whether a fake website is being used to trick people

You do not need to become a DNS expert today.

Just remember this:

DNS helps your device find websites by name.

That is enough for now.

## Basic Router Security Settings

Your router is one of the most important security devices in your home.

It controls how your devices connect to the internet.

It also helps decide who is allowed onto your network.

That means weak router settings can create real security problems.

The good news is:

You do not need to be an expert to understand the basics.

---

## Router Admin Password

Your router admin password is the password used to log in to the router settings page.

This is different from your Wi-Fi password.

The router admin password controls access to the router's control panel.

If someone gets access to the router settings, they may be able to:
- change network settings
- view connected devices
- change Wi-Fi information
- weaken security settings

If your router still uses a default admin password, that should be changed.

A default password is one that came with the device.

Examples might include:
- admin
- password
- admin123

Those are not safe.

---

## Wi-Fi Password

Your Wi-Fi password is the password people use to connect devices to your wireless network.

A good Wi-Fi password should be:
- long
- unique
- hard to guess
- not reused from another account

Avoid passwords based on:
- your name
- your address
- your phone number
- your birthday
- your pet's name
- the word "password"

A stronger example would look more like a phrase:

```text
CoffeeRiverWindow72!
```

You do not need to use that exact password.

The point is to use something longer and harder to guess.

---

## WPA2 And WPA3

Wi-Fi security settings often use terms like:

```text
WPA2
WPA3
```

These are security protections for wireless networks.

For most homes:
- WPA3 is best if your router supports it
- WPA2 is still commonly used and acceptable
- WEP should not be used

If you see WEP listed as your Wi-Fi security type, that is outdated and unsafe.

---

## Firmware Updates

Your router runs software called firmware.

Firmware is the built-in software that helps the router operate.

Router manufacturers sometimes release firmware updates to fix bugs and security problems.

Many routers update automatically.

Some require you to check manually.

Look for sections like:
- Firmware Update
- Router Update
- Software Update
- System Update

Do not install updates from random websites.

Only use the router dashboard, router app, or the manufacturer's official website.

---

## Guest Network

A guest network is a separate Wi-Fi network for visitors or temporary devices.

This can be useful because guests do not need access to your main home network.

A guest network is good for:
- visitors
- smart home devices
- temporary devices
- devices you do not fully trust

Example:

```text
Main Wi-Fi: SmithFamilyWiFi
Guest Wi-Fi: SmithGuest
```

Using a guest network can help reduce risk.

It creates a little more separation inside your home network.
